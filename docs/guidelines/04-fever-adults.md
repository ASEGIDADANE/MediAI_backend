# Fever in adults (general guidance)

## Red flags — seek urgent care at a hospital or emergency unit now

- Fever with confusion, difficulty waking, or seizures
- Severe headache with stiff neck
- Difficulty breathing or chest pain
- Rash that does not fade with pressure (non-blanching)
- Signs of severe dehydration (very little urine, dizziness, fainting)
- Fever after recent hospitalization, chemotherapy, or weak immune system

## When to see a clinician soon (within 24–48 hours)

- Fever above 38.5°C (101.3°F) lasting more than 3 days without improvement
- Fever returning after it seemed to improve
- Localized severe pain (ear, throat, abdomen, urinary symptoms)
- Pregnancy and fever (seek assessment promptly)

## General information

- Fever is often the body’s response to infection. Rest and fluids help many mild illnesses.
- Paracetamol or ibuprofen may reduce discomfort if you already use them safely and a clinician has not advised against them. Do not exceed package directions.
- In Ethiopia, malaria can cause fever in some regions — if you live in or recently visited a malaria-risk area, tell a clinician and get tested at a health facility rather than assuming the cause.

## What this chat cannot do

- This is educational information, not a diagnosis. Testing and treatment decisions belong with a licensed clinician at a health center or hospital.
